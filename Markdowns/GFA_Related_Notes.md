## Timeline of GFA Concessions

### Detailed Policy Timeline

| Date | Policy Change | Remarks |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Apr 1988 | Initial recognition of basic GFA exemptions | Early administrative recognition of non-accountable GFA components such as arcades, covered walkways, verandahs and essential plant rooms. Primary objective was improving urban design and pedestrian circulation rather than sustainability. |
| Jan 1990 | Expansion to residential amenities | Formal recognition of balconies and utility/drying areas as exempted features. Marked a shift towards improving residential livability and housing quality. |
| Nov 1997 | Standardisation and expansion of exempted features | Clarified treatment of bay windows, void spaces and prefabricated external features. Greater emphasis on modern building design and façade articulation. |
| Feb 2001 | JPN 1 – Introduction of Green and Innovative Features | Major policy turning point. GFA concessions began to encourage environmentally friendly and innovative building design. Introduced concessions for features such as sky gardens, wider corridors, lift lobbies, acoustic fins and ventilation-enhancing elements. |
| Feb 2002 | JPN 2 – Expansion of Green Features | Extended the Green and Innovative Features framework to include utility platforms, prefabricated external walls, noise barriers and additional green features. Expanded the role of GFA concessions as an environmental policy instrument. |
| Aug 2003 | JPN 3 (First Issue) | Strengthened coordination among the Buildings Department, Lands Department and Planning Department. Began integrating planning, land lease and building control considerations into GFA concession administration. |
| Jul 2005 | PNAP Revision (Appendix E) | First consolidated catalogue of GFA concessions and exclusions. Standardised the treatment of exempted features and became the principal technical reference for later revisions. |
| Mar 2007 | PNAP Revision | Introduced stronger performance-based assessment and technical justification requirements for obtaining GFA concessions. Marked a shift from feature-based approval toward performance-based regulation. |
| Nov 2008 | JPN 4 (First Issue) | Harmonised GFA accountability across the Buildings Department, Lands Department and Planning Department. Introduced the Group I/II/III framework, making GFA administration more systematic and consistent. |
| Feb 2009 | PNAP Revision | Introduced the BD106 conditions system and lifecycle compliance requirements, allowing GFA concessions to be monitored from approval through project completion. |
| May 2010 | ADM-2 Appendix G introduced | Comprehensive categorisation of GFA concessions into different classes, including Green Features and amenity features. Consolidated technical guidance under a unified framework. |
| Sept 2010 | Mandatory disclosure requirements | Developers were required to disclose GFA concessions for completed developments, increasing market transparency. |
| Jan–Apr 2011 | New ADM-2 regime implemented | Revised administrative framework applied to new building plan submissions, reflecting the Government's broader reform package. |
| 2011 | Comprehensive GFA Concession Reform Package | Most significant reform of the concession system. Introduced an overall cap (generally around 10%) on many discretionary GFA concessions, together with stricter approval requirements. |
| 2011 | Sustainable Building Design (SBD) Guidelines introduced | Building Separation, Building Setback and Site Coverage of Greenery (SCG) became integrated into the approval framework for GFA concessions, linking urban design requirements with development incentives. |
| 2011 | Enhanced transparency and legal controls | Mandatory disclosure in sales brochures, Deed of Mutual Covenant (DMC) designation, and Land Registry registration for relevant concession areas strengthened governance and public transparency. |
| 2011 | BEAM Plus integration | Certain categories of GFA concessions became conditional upon satisfying BEAM Plus assessment requirements, shifting the system toward performance-based environmental assessment. |
| 2011–2019 | Progressive refinement of Green Features | Ongoing revisions clarified requirements relating to ventilation, sky gardens, greenery provision, plant rooms and other environmentally friendly building features. |
| Apr 2019 | JPN 3 Revision | Further strengthened and clarified Site Coverage of Greenery (SCG) requirements within the Sustainable Building Design framework. |
| May 2019 | JPN 5 | Streamlined the treatment of Building Height Restrictions (BHR), improving coordination between planning controls and building administration. |
| Sept 2019 | JPN 6 | Updated and consolidated Sustainable Building Design requirements, including Building Separation, Building Setback and Site Coverage of Greenery, within the GFA concession framework. |
| 23 Aug 2021 | JPN 7 | Further aligned Site Coverage (SC) requirements across planning, lease administration and building control, improving regulatory consistency. |
| 18 Oct 2021 | Revised JPN 4 | Clarified the Buildings Department's role as the primary authority for verifying GFA calculations, further strengthening cross-departmental coordination. |
| 1 Aug 2022 | JPN 8 – Modular Integrated Construction (MiC) | Major innovation-oriented reform. Introduced dedicated incentives for MiC, including additional GFA exemptions (outside the general concession cap), Site Coverage exemptions and limited Building Height relaxation to encourage adoption of advanced construction technologies. |
| 2022 | JPN 2 updated | MiC-related provisions were separated into JPN 8, simplifying and modernising the Green Features framework. |
| Mar 2022 | ADM-2 Revision | Administrative updates and procedural refinements. No major new categories of GFA concessions were introduced. |

## Sustainable Building Guidelines (2011)
* Transfer_hkre > clarence > APP-152

### APP-152 (2011)
Introduced the Sustainable Building Design (SBD) Guidelines, which focus on three key aspects of urban design:
1. Building Separation
2. Building Setback
3. Site Coverage of Greenery (SCG)
The guidelines combine policy objectives with technical design requirements aimed at improving:
- Air ventilation
- Pedestrian environment
- Urban greenery
- Overall urban sustainability
Developers may also adopt performance-based designs supported by technical studies (e.g. Air Ventilation Assessment or Computational Fluid Dynamics studies), where appropriate.

### APP-152 (2016 Revision)
- Refined and clarified the technical implementation of the SBD Guidelines.
- Updated assessment procedures and technical requirements.
- No fundamental change to the underlying policy objectives introduced in 2011.

### Policy Implications
1. The SBD Guidelines became part of the approval framework for certain discretionary GFA concessions introduced under the 2011 reform package.
2. Prior to 2011, many Green Features could obtain GFA concessions without being subject to a comprehensive Sustainable Building Design framework.
3. Following the 2011 reforms, developers seeking certain discretionary GFA concessions generally had to demonstrate compliance with the SBD Guidelines (together with other applicable requirements).
4. The SBD Guidelines do **not** themselves grant additional GFA. Rather, they form one of the eligibility requirements for obtaining certain discretionary GFA concessions.
5. The SBD Guidelines primarily affect discretionary Green Features and do not apply to all statutory or mandatory GFA exemptions.

## BEAM Plus Policy Design (2011)
### APP-151 (2011)
Introduced a comprehensive policy package that links:
1. SBD
2. GFA Concessions
3. Energy Efficiency
Formalising that GFA concessions are conditional on sustainability performance. 

#### Features: 
* Compliance with SBD before any GFA concessions are granted
* Overall concessions are capped at 10% of GFA with some exemptions (See Segment below)
* BEAM Plus
* Energy reporting system introduced.
* Mandatory disclosure of energy performance data and BEAM results

### APP-151 (2014)
- Integration with APP-156 (energy efficiency). Must comply, additional layer of control beyond BEAM
- Additional energy submissions required. (Provisional energy efficiency report, final efficiency report)
- Stronger Link to building Services Performance (more engineering based, less volunatary based)

### APP-151 (2019)
- Extended submission for BEAM submission 
- Stronger Integration with APP-156. (Energy compliance is now fully embedded requirment)
- Expanded FGA concession categories (MiC features included in Appendix A)
- Inclusion of GFA concessions from JPN-based features

### APP-151 (Aug 2020)
- Updated GFA concession list to include JPN-1 and JPN-2 Features, updated plant room classification
- Early inclusion of MiC related features (transitional form)

### APP-151 (Dec 2020)
- MiC not fully separated, still appears as an additional green feature, embedded under broader concession categories. 

### APP-151 (2022)
- Integrated JPN8 (MiC Policy)
- Clear separation of concession categories. (MiC gets its own category now) 
- Stronger environmental systems recognition (eg. Rainwater harvesting, Solar system, Energy systems)
- Categorieses sky gardens, horizontal screens, ducts and plant distribution

### APP-151 (2023)
- Implemented a post-2017 review of GFA concessions mechanism -> strengthens the link between GFA concessions and quality built environment outcomes
- Expanded the pre-requsites for GFA concessions
    1. SBD
    2. Energy Compliance
    3. BEAM Plus registration + certification
    4. Target rating requirement (Gold, Silver, Bronze)
- Developers must declare a target (Gold, Silver, Bronze): GFA concessions depend on achieved sustainability level, not just participation. 
- Massive expansion of design requirments in Appendix C (eg. BIM submission rules, MiMEP >= 50% coverage)
- Stronger legal + compliance framework + Multi-stage submission and approval process. 

### APP-151 (2024)
- Refinement of GFA concession categories 
- Alignment with updated technical standards

### Overview of BEAM Plus
Purpose of BEAM Plus is to set out a series of best practices (above statutory requirements) that enable the building industry to create more sustainable buildings. By sustainable, it means that the buildings would foster the health and wellbeing of human beings while caring for the local and global environments.
* Includes an assessment to rank developments on their ability to meet statutory requirments (Gold/ Silver/ Bronze)
