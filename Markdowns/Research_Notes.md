# Housing Construction and Uncertainties in high-rise city (2018) Habitat International
### Ka-hung Yu and Eddie Chi-man Hui

* Rate of growth of (A/B/C) PPI is greater than the rate of growth of Luxury PPI. 
* PRI observes a more modest increase. 
* There is a sharp decline in the volume of new residential units over the GFC period, and the industry has not made a full recovery. 
* Policies of Town Planning Ordinance and Building (Planning) Applications results in a lengthier planning and application process that postpones the actual construction of housing units. 
    * Land-use zoning system (1997)
    * Development planning system (1997)
    * Believed to be source of higher development costs. 
        * Housing prices increase when there is lower land supply or regulations that minimise lot size and restrain development density. (Brueckner 1990)
        * Development controls create an amenity that is more costly to develop, meaning higher prices for the finished product.

* Developer-initiated land exchange can also generate additional land for residential deverlopment (follows previous research paper)
* Developers preferences to land sites for sale (purpose determined by the government prior to sale) are reflected by the bidding price. 
* Reasons why developers may not increasing housing supply: 
    * Market uncertainties rendering present development less-than-optimal 
    * Developers are not responding to the upward housing price movement 
    * Innate uncertainties of the planning control system. 

* 4 stages of Residential Development 
    1. Planning control process under the jurisdiction of the Town Planning Board 
    2. Building plan approval process under the jusrisdiction of the Building Authority 
    3. Developers' decision to commence construction 
    4. Construction process 
    Property developers have the most control over the third phase only 

* Land Exchange mechanism: 
    * A developer surrenders an 'old lot' in exchange for a 'new lot'. 
    * In-situ land exchange: New land partially overlaps with the old lot 
    * Non-in-situ: old land site is not overlapping in any way. 
    * Land exchange encourages property developers to utilise their land banks for housing constructions. -> Concessions by the government 
    * Modification of lease conditions applicable to the 'old' site allows for the creation of land substitutes for the development of more living space. 

#### Key argument: The differences between land sale and land exchange along with the 'hybrid' planning control system make property developers' decisions as to when to initiate housing construction more complicated.

* Land Banking breaks down the relationship between land supply and residential property prices. 
* Land use zoning and planning regulations dictate responsiveness of developers to housing price movements. 
    * This also cause uncertainties in the stochastic development controls that surround land exchange. 
On the optimal timing of land development:
* Theory: If the discounted future income stream exceeds the development costs, land development will occur in the present.
    * Usage of two-period and multi-period models. 
* Theory: Arrow-Fisher-Henry Analysis indicates that should land development be both indivisible and irreversible, the owner of a land site, by deciding to develop a piece of land in the present, essentially overlooks the benefits of waiting. 
* Theory: When the rate of return of the investment exceeds the combination of discount rate, property tax rate, and an uncertainty premium, it would be the optimal time to develop a particular land site. 
    * These theories, with the concept of discounting, naturally ropes in the effect of interest rates. 

#### Overall aim: When to develop a plot of land from a Macro perspective. 
1. Whether property developers' decisions to develop land site for residential purpose coincides with housing price movements
2. also examine other construction and development control-related factors 

Key Argument: Developer will commence development if the net present value is positive
* Viability of residential development project = f(Property Price, Interest Rate, Construction Costs, Uncertainties)
* GFA = f(Property Price, Uncertainty(Price), Interest Rate, Uncertainty (Interest), Construction, Uncertainty (construction), Land Supply)
+ The Rider Levett Bucknall Tender Price Index is used in measuring the cost of construction 

#### Methodology used: Vector Error Correction Model (VECM) which enables impulse response analysis and variance decomponsition analysis. 
* Enables researchers to find out the respective impacts of the selected variables on the amount of housing construction to be commmenced by the developers
1. How developers' decisions to initiate housing construction in a particular period of time are infleucned by innovations in the other selected variables 
2. The amount of GFA variance to be explained by other variables in the span of 3 years

#### Results
* Property Price Movements 
    * GFA responds positively to innovations in property price volatilities -> When housing price movements become more volatile, the initial reaction from the developers is to take a risk-averse position by commencing development sooner. 
    * GFA responds negatively to innovations in expected property price growth -> As Expected Property Price growth rate increases, developers have the tendency to withold housing development.
* Construction Cost Movements 
    * Initial response upon a positive shock in construction cost is negative -> Developers tend to withhold construction. 
    * Many Hong Kong Developers serve as price searchers rather than as price takers. -> These developers can simply sell the completed housing units at a higher price to cover the soaring construction costs. 
    * Property developers tend to withhold development for the time being, until the expected grwoth in construction costs develerates 
    * In the short run, developers tend to commence construction sooner as construction cost movements become increasingly volatile. Yet, should such volatilities remain, they have he tendency to withhold developement. 
* Interest Rate Movements 
    * Effect of actual interest rate movements on development timing can be said as far from certain: 
        * Short Run: Delay 
        * Medium term: Commence
        * Long run: Delay 
    How does the study differentiate long run and short run? 
    * Expectation that the Federal Funds Rate is going to rise: Developers will initiate construction sooner. 
    * Uncertainties in interest rate movements initially lead to the withholding of construction by developers. 
    * Should developers not be certain as to the changes in interest payment incurred during the construction phase, they are likely to hold off developments 
    * Uncertainties in interest rate movements yield a much higher impact than the expected interest rate changes. 
* Land supply situations
    * Accumulated responses to an increase in housing supply in the last 12 months are uncertain. 
        * Developers tend to initially commence their projects sooner upon the emergence of such shocks. 
        * The fluctuating responses towards land supply movements, if anything, reinforce the notion that the relationship between land supply and housing supply is not as certain as most people perceive. 
        * Concern among developers towards over-construction of housing as a response to the higher land supply in the short rum, which exert a downward pressure to housing prices when the construction is completed. 
        * Should a higher proportion of residential land supply comes from the developer-initiated land exchange, the uncertainties in Hong Kong's development control system rise due to delays caused by developers' negotiation with the Lands Department. 

Conclusions: 
* US monetary policies have helped to create conditions that increased housing demand that leads to soaring housing prices.

# The Costs of Housing Regulation: Evidence from Generative Regulatory Measurement. Working Paper. 2925
### Alexander W. Bartik, Arpit Gupta, Daniel Milo 

### Introduction

* Focus: Zoning laws; Land use policies 
* Method Used: Large Language MOdels to ingest, classify and respond to continuous questions. Principal Component Analysis. Model of inter-municipal competition. 
* Location: California (to ensure that results are not geographically biased)
* Questions: A set of reglatory questions initially developed by the Pioneer Institute for Massachusetts. Further expanded by the research team. 

##### Principal Components: 
1. Associated with high prices and high construction --> Regulations that allow development but exact value in high-demand environments 
    1. Loads heavily on regulations that allow local governments to extract and redistribute housing surplus. 
    2. Regulations are typically found in densely populated, centrally located cities that are Democratic in nature. 
    3. Regulations are higher in areas with higher amenitites 
    3. These regulations emerge precisely in locations with the most valuable land and highest development potential
2. Exclusionary zoning Practices aimed at limiting density and affordable housing options. 
    1. Associates with high prices but low construction
    2. Regulations that restrict housing supply
    3. Loads heavily on bulk regulations, such as minimum lot size requirements, and procedural barriers to development 
    4. Typically in affluent, predominantly white suburban areas that tend to lean Republican
    5. Strong correlation between these regulatory measures and local school performance, social mobility, suggesting a role in educational sorting. 

##### Model: Inter-municipal competition: 
* Local governments strategically and non-cooperatively select between two sets of housing regulations: one associated with value capture and the other with exclusionary zoning thorugh the imposition of minimum housing requirements. 

##### Results:
1. Inter-municipal competition model predicts that metropolitan cores with high amenitites will implement value capture regulations. 
2. Model predicts that suburban areas will adopt exclusionary regulations to generate more tax revenue from a concentrated pool of affluent households. 
3. Exclusionary zoning is less attractive to municipalities that are too large to be filled primarily with the most affluent households. 

##### Contributions: 
1. Methological approach: Developing a general-purpose approach to measuring the content of regulatory documents and quantify the accuracy and reliability of the resulting classifications. 
2. Production of a novel dataset on housing regulation across the United States. 
3. Provide a more nuanced portrait of zoning regulations than was previously possible. 
4. Theory contribution is to develop a model of non-cooperative regulation choice by local governments who have access to multiple zoning instruments to explain the zoning patterns in the observed data. 

### Data

##### Background: 
1. Zoning broadly consists of land use regulation: partitioning local land into distinct use classes and bulk regulations, which regulate the physical dimensions and density of buildings in different land use classes. 
    + Example of bulk regulation: minimum lot sizes 
2. Zoning codes specify the process thorugh which development is approved. 
3. Municipalities enforce laws by issuing municipal codes which outline local regulation in different domains. 

##### Obtaining Data: 
1. Text of municipal codes are sourced from a variety of municipal aggregators including American Legal Publishing, Municode and Ordinance.com
2. Covers 25% of all municipalities in the U.S. abd 6% of all townships. 
3. Combines these municipal ordinances with a variety of municipal level demographic, housing and economic information

### Processing Municipal Codes using LLMs
Challenge: Many ordinances have lengths that exceed the context window usable by current LLMs
Solution: Use Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

CHallenge: To prove that the LLM is accurate and reliable in understanding knowledge
* Solution: 
    + Begin with the questions from Pioneer Institute and some additional questions of their own
    + RAG ranks the most relevant information needed to answer the question. (There is also a cross-encoder step to rerank the top 50 chunks)
    + Selects the top 4000 tokens. 
    + First there are binary (Yes, No) questions. 
    + Next there are open-ended arguments. 
* Improvement steps: 
    + Prompt Chaining
    + Prompt Engineering
    + Providing detailed question background information. 
    + Trained on a distinct subsample of the Pioneer Data

##### Model Validation
* Continuous Data -> Correlation with ground truth of export classifications after winsorisation of results at the 1% level. 
* Binary Qustions -> Accuracy metric + Relative Squared Error (RSE)

### Characterising Housing Regulations

##### Principal Component Analysis
Aim: To summarise the correlation structure between regulations
Dataset contains 20 questions and coveres nearly 6000 local governments

Highlighted Features
* Continuous measures reveal substantial variation in regulatory scope and complexity across municipalities
* Average municipality has 14 zoning districts and bulk regulation are frequently stringent. 
* Housing process regulations vary substantially across different regions. 
* Binary regulatory controls: Multifamily housing is prohibited in 5% of jurisdictions, 86% of municipalities restrict conversions to multifamily units, 37% ban mixed-use developments
* Average absolute value correlation structure between variables is only 0.08, with near zero correlation between many regulations. 

Steps of the PCA
1. Checking how many principal components using the elbow method
2. Velicer's Minimum Average partial test suggests that 2 principal components minimise the objective function. 
3. Decoding the principal components:
    1. Show associations between housing construction, housing cost and the two key principal components 
    2. Interpret the association with housing construction and cost in light of a simple framework of supply and demand
4. Areas with a high value of the first principal component have high house prices as well as construction, while those low in PC1 have low prices and low construction
5. Second principal component associates highly with areas that have high house prices but low construction
